# VI. The Divergence from Torah Observance
![Answering the question: Did Jesus, the apostles, Paul, and the New Testament Church keep the Torah?](../assets/The%20apostles%20and%20the%20torah%20-%20Reconstructing%20the%20first%20century%20church.jpg)

## 1. Historical Turning Point
The earliest followers of Yeshua continued to live as Jews loyal to the Torah. The separation between Judaism and the emerging Gentile church was gradual, not immediate. The first decisive rupture came not from theology but from politics and survival. After the destruction of the Temple in 70 CE and especially following the Bar Kokhba Revolt (132‚Äì135 CE), Roman authorities outlawed Jewish practices such as Sabbath observance and circumcision. Communities that could plausibly distance themselves from ‚ÄúJewish customs‚Äù gained a political advantage (1).

It was under these pressures that certain Gentile assemblies began re-interpreting apostolic writings to justify divergence. Doctrinal adaptation became a survival mechanism. To avoid persecution, many re-cast the faith in a Hellenistic philosophical mold, redefining ‚ÄúLaw‚Äù as antithetical to ‚ÄúGrace.‚Äù

For detailed exegesis of Colossians 2, Galatians 4, Romans 14, and Hebrews 4 as commonly misunderstood ‚Äúcalendar‚Äù passages, see **Supplemental Study IV: Calendar Misreadings**.

## 2. From Hebraic to Hellenistic Framework
The apostolic worldview was Hebraic, rooted in covenant, land, and communal obedience. Hellenistic converts, educated in Platonic dualism, interpreted Scripture through a metaphysical lens that devalued the material. Body and spirit became opposing categories; physical obedience was deemed inferior to ‚Äúspiritual‚Äù worship. As N. T. Wright notes, ‚ÄúWhen the gospel entered the Greco-Roman world, it was refracted through categories foreign to Israel‚Äôs covenantal narrative.‚Äù (2)

This shift turned Torah observance into a symbol of bondage to the material world. Platonic influence, reinforced by Stoic and Gnostic tendencies, equated true enlightenment with detachment from ritual and the senses. Consequently, dietary laws, Sabbaths, and festivals were re-interpreted allegorically rather than practiced literally.

## 3. Emergence of Sunday Observance
The move from Sabbath to Sunday illustrates this trajectory. The earliest evidence of first-day worship appears in the Didache 14 (ca. 100 CE) and Ignatius‚Äôs *Magnesians* 9:1. Both reflect communities under pressure to differentiate from Judaism after 70 CE. Ignatius contrasts ‚Äúthose who lived according to the old order‚Äù with ‚Äúthose who live according to the Lord‚Äôs Day.‚Äù His intent was apologetic, not theological ‚Äî to assure Roman observers that believers were not participating in the proscribed Sabbath assemblies (3).

The pattern continued with Justin Martyr (c. 150 CE), who redefined Sabbath as allegory: ‚ÄúThe new law requires you to keep a perpetual Sabbath‚Äù (*Dialogue with Trypho* 12). This hermeneutic severed the historical command from its practice. The theological justification for Sunday thus post-dates the apostles by nearly a century.

## 4. The Council of Nicaea and the Passover Controversy
The divergence reached formal codification in 325 CE when Constantine convened the Council of Nicaea. Among its aims was to detach the Christian calendar from Jewish reckoning. The Quartodeciman controversy concerned whether Pascha (Easter) should coincide with the biblical Passover. Constantine decreed a separate calculation: ‚ÄúLet us have nothing in common with the detestable Jewish crowd.‚Äù (4)

This statement reveals the theological anti-Judaism already entrenched. The festival that had once memorialized Yeshua‚Äôs Passover sacrifice was recast according to solar cycles, replacing the lunar-based biblical calendar. Thus, calendrical reform became a tool of ecclesial identity and imperial cohesion.

## 5. Marcion and the Rejection of the Old Testament
The second century also witnessed overt repudiation of the Hebrew Scriptures. Marcion of Sinope (c. 140 CE) proposed a canon excluding the Old Testament and heavily editing Paul‚Äôs letters. He portrayed the God of Israel as a lesser deity of justice, distinct from the Father of Jesus. Though condemned as heresy, Marcion‚Äôs dualism profoundly influenced Christian consciousness. His dichotomy between Law and Gospel became embedded, albeit in moderated form, in later orthodoxy (5).

Ironically, defenders of orthodoxy adopted portions of Marcion‚Äôs rhetoric to argue that Torah observance was obsolete ‚Äî a case of heresy shaping the mainstream it opposed.

## 6. The Role of Anti-Jewish Legislation
Roman imperial policy further incentivized Gentile distinction. Hadrian‚Äôs edicts (135 CE) banned circumcision and Torah study. Later, Theodosius I (391 CE) prohibited Jewish conversion efforts and synagogue construction. Christian theologians responded by redefining the covenant to exclude Israel as a living entity. Augustine‚Äôs ‚Äúwitness people‚Äù doctrine confined Jews to perpetual exile as testimony to Christian truth (6).

These political dynamics reinforced theological detachment. Observance of Torah markers ‚Äî Sabbath, circumcision, dietary distinction ‚Äî became stigmatized as Judaizing. The apostolic halakhah was replaced by ecclesial canons enforcing uniform Sunday worship and pork consumption as symbols of liberty.

## 7. The Greek Philosophical Recast
Hellenistic philosophy transposed Torah categories into metaphysical abstractions. Law became ‚Äúnature‚Äôs order,‚Äù sin ‚Äúignorance,‚Äù and salvation ‚Äúgnosis.‚Äù The moral concreteness of Torah ‚Äî specific times, foods, and rituals ‚Äî was replaced with universal ideals. Clement of Alexandria (c. 200 CE) spiritualized the commandments; Origen allegorized Israel‚Äôs festivals as stages of the soul‚Äôs ascent.

Such interpretations, while intellectually sophisticated, redefined obedience as contemplation. As Daniel Boyarin observes, ‚ÄúChristian theology became the translation of Israel‚Äôs Scripture into Greek metaphysics.‚Äù (7) The lived practice of Torah disappeared into symbolism.

## 8. Rabbinic and Christian Parting of the Ways
Parallel to this development, rabbinic Judaism consolidated its identity through the *Mishnah* (c. 200 CE). Both traditions sought boundary maintenance in response to trauma ‚Äî the loss of the Temple and Roman persecution. The rabbis excluded ‚Äú*minim*‚Äù (sectarians, often Jewish believers) from the synagogue liturgy; the church reciprocated with creeds excluding ‚ÄúJudaizers.‚Äù The result was mutual excommunication of two sibling movements that had once shared table fellowship.

Modern scholars such as James Dunn describe this process as ‚Äúthe parting of the ways.‚Äù Yet, as he notes, ‚ÄúIt was not a theological necessity but a historical contingency.‚Äù (8) The apostles themselves would have recognized neither side‚Äôs eventual formulations.

## 9. Survival of Torah-Faithful Communities
Despite marginalization, Torah-observant communities persisted. The Nazarenes and Ebionites retained Sabbath, dietary laws, and festivals well into the fourth century. Epiphanius (c. 375 CE) reports that they ‚Äúconfess Jesus as the Christ yet are still bound to the Law‚Äù (*Panarion* 29). His tone is derisive, but the testimony confirms their continuity with apostolic practice (9).

Archaeological evidence from the Golan and Transjordan corroborates mixed Jewish-Christian synagogues where inscriptions invoke Yeshua alongside Torah texts. These groups eventually disappeared under Byzantine persecution and Islamic expansion, but their existence demonstrates that Torah observance remained a legitimate Christian expression for centuries.

## 10. Summary of Section VI
- The divergence from Torah observance resulted from historical pressure, not apostolic teaching.  
- Hellenistic philosophy and Roman politics reshaped the faith into a Gentile religion.  
- Sunday observance and Easter dating were imperial reforms, not apostolic decrees.  
- Marcion‚Äôs dualism and anti-Jewish legislation entrenched replacement theology.  
- Yet Torah-faithful believers ‚Äî the Nazarenes and others ‚Äî preserved the apostolic heritage.

The ‚Äúparting of the ways‚Äù was thus a tragedy of history, not the intention of the apostles. Their vision of the renewed covenant ‚Äî a Jewish Messiah leading a Torah-faithful, Spirit-filled community of Jews and Gentiles ‚Äî was eclipsed but not extinguished.
